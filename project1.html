Project Overview

Hierarchical Optimization Technique for Placement of Visual Landmarks

Problem: Vision-based indoor service robots frequently suffer from "spatial ambiguity" in repetitive environments (such as corridors). They struggle to maintain their exact location, with a critical point of failure occurring when they lose sight of the walls while rotating at intersections.

Scope: The project focuses on developing a highly optimized, cost-effective navigation system using ArUco fiducial markers. The scope spans the entire development pipeline: mathematical modeling, 3D digital-twin simulations, and final physical hardware validation on a mobile robot in indoor corridor environments.

Technical Stack

Computer Vision MATLAB Webots NVIDIA Jetson Nano

Methodology

The project's workflow transitions from baseline testing to a mathematically calculated deployment strategy. Initially, the environment was modeled by placing markers manually in a dense, uniform layout along the walls, acting as a baseline pool of 178 candidate locations.

Testing this manual approach revealed significant flaws. A dense layout created excessive visual clutter and burdened the robot’s computer vision system with processing too many overlapping tags simultaneously. Furthermore, despite the high volume of tags, the manual layout still failed at junctions, as the robot would turn and face blank wall space, losing its localization.

Hierarchical Optimization Algorithm

To resolve the inefficiencies inferred from the manual placement, an algorithm was introduced to calculate the exact, minimum necessary coordinates for the markers:

  • Stage 1 (Functional Zones): Tags are first assigned to destination points (like doorways) to guarantee precise localization when the robot stops to execute a task.
  • Stage 2 (Junctions): Tags are explicitly calculated for corners (L-turns and T-turns) to ensure the robot has guaranteed "pre-turn" and "post-turn" visibility, directly fixing the rotation blind spots.
  • Stage 3 (Straight Corridors): A greedy heuristic algorithm filters the remaining straight-line gaps. It scores locations by maximizing new path visibility while mathematically penalizing placements that are unnecessarily close to other tags.

Why these are used: Manual placement relies on inefficient guesswork that overwhelms the system hardware. The three-stage algorithm is utilized to mathematically guarantee uninterrupted localization, drastically reduce physical infrastructure, and keep camera processing loads at an optimal minimum.

Outcomes & Comparisons

The optimized algorithm was tested and compared against the initial dense candidate pool, yielding the following results:

  • Infrastructure Reduction: The optimization algorithm successfully filtered the initial manual pool of 178 candidate locations down to a final layout of just 32 tags. This represents an 82% reduction in physical markers.
  • Computational Efficiency: By reducing the layout, the camera observed an optimal average of 1.011 tags per frame. This completely eliminated the processing overload seen in the dense manual phase.
  • Turning Success: The targeted junction placements completely resolved the cornering issue, achieving a 100% pre-turn success rate and preventing the robot from ever getting lost during rotation.
  • Localization Reliability: Even with 82% fewer tags, the maximum "blackout" distance (gaps where the robot sees no tags) was minimized to just 0.3 meters. The system maintained a highly reliable 94.2% localization availability throughout the entire mapped environment.

Project Overview

Detection of Red-Palm Weevil Infestation in Coconut Trees Using Neural Network Systems

Problem: The red palm weevil (RPW) causes severe, irreversible damage to coconut palms by feeding inside the tree trunk. Traditional visual inspections are highly labor-intensive and largely ineffective for early detection because visible symptoms (like yellowing fronds) only appear during the advanced, fatal stages of infestation.

Scope: The project aims to develop an automated, non-invasive acoustic detection system using neural networks. This system identifies early-stage infestations in real-time by capturing and analyzing the characteristic audio frequencies emitted by RPW larvae feeding inside the trees.

Technical Stack

Methodology

The project transitions pest control from reactive visual inspection to proactive acoustic monitoring, utilizing audio frequency analysis and deep learning to identify the characteristic sounds of RPW larvae.

Acoustic Analysis Pipeline

  • Data Processing & Denoising: Utilizes Mel Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCC) and spectral contrast features. Wavelet denoising filters out environmental noise while preserving critical larval sound signatures.
  • Segmentation & Balancing: Audio files are segmented based on RMS energy levels to isolate useful signals. SMOTE is applied to address severe data imbalance and generate synthetic samples for the clean/infested minority class.
  • Neural Network Architecture: A custom PyTorch Feedforward Neural Network (FFNN) serves as the core classifier, passing scaled features through three dense hidden layers (512, 256, 128 neurons) utilizing ReLU and Sigmoid activations for deep pattern abstraction.

Outcomes & Results

The FFNN model was rigorously evaluated and compared against several traditional and state-of-the-art machine learning models:

  • Overall Accuracy: The proposed neural network achieved an overall testing accuracy of 85.06% and a training accuracy of 94.04%.
  • Optimized for Safety (High Recall): Achieved a critical high recall rate of 0.80 for the infested class, meaning it successfully identified 80% of all truly infested trees, minimizing dangerous "false negatives" where an infestation might be missed.
  • Superiority Over Ensembles: In comparative analysis, the PyTorch FFNN outperformed advanced ensemble algorithms including XGBoost, CatBoost, LightGBM, Gradient Boosting, and AdaBoost, delivering the highest macro-averaged F1-score of 0.81.
